1 What is this tool?

The Solar UAV Endurance Estimator helps you answer one simple question:

💡
The Big Question

"Can my drone fly forever on solar power alone — and if not, how long can it fly before the battery runs out?"

You enter information about your drone (solar panels, motors, battery) and where/when you want to fly. The tool instantly calculates how much solar energy is available, how much power your drone needs, and whether the balance is positive or negative.

Who is it for?

  • Drone designers — figure out what size solar panel your drone needs
  • Mission planners — find the best time of day and season to fly
  • Students & researchers — learn how solar energy and drone power work together
  • Enthusiasts — explore "what if" scenarios without any coding

2 How to get started (5 steps)

Follow these steps in order for your first result:

  1. Pick a solar cell type — At the top of the page, click one of the preset buttons like SunPower Maxeon or Mono c-Si. This fills in the panel efficiency settings automatically. Not sure which to pick? Start with Mono c-Si — it's the most common type.
  2. Set your panel size — Enter how many square metres of solar panels your drone has. If you're just exploring, start with 2.5 m² as a typical medium-size UAV.
  3. Set your location — Enter the latitude of where you'll fly (positive = north of equator, negative = south). You can also click on the map. Choose the month of your mission. Noon (12.0) is a good starting time.
  4. Enter motor power — Set how many motors your drone has and how many Watts each motor uses at cruise speed. If you're unsure, 80 W per motor is a reasonable starting estimate for a medium fixed-wing drone.
  5. Enter battery info — Set your battery capacity in Wh and choose the chemistry type. A 500 Wh LiPo is a typical medium-size pack.

After step 5, look at the coloured Verdict banner — it will tell you instantly whether your design works. Read on to understand all the results in detail.

3 Filling in the inputs

The tool has 5 input panels. Here is what every field means in plain English.

Panel 1 — Solar Array (Your Solar Panels)

area m² default: 2.5
The total surface area of your solar panels in square metres. For a flying wing drone, this is the area of the wing top surface that is actually covered with solar cells — not the full wing area. A small hobby drone might have 0.5 m², a large surveillance UAV might have 5–10 m².
efficiency % default: 22.0
How good the solar cells are at converting sunlight into electricity. Higher = better. A value of 22% means 22% of the light energy hitting the panel becomes electrical power. This value is printed on the cell datasheet as "η" (eta) or "conversion efficiency". Typical range: 14% (thin film) to 29% (high-end GaAs).
temp_coeff %/°C default: −0.35
How much efficiency is lost for every degree Celsius above 25°C. This is always a negative number — panels get slightly worse as they heat up. A value of −0.35 means if the panel is 10°C hotter than 25°C (i.e., 35°C), efficiency drops by 3.5%. Found on the datasheet as "temperature coefficient of Pmax".
panel_temp °C default: 45.0
The actual temperature of the panel cells during flight. When flying, airflow cools the panels — so 35–50°C is typical. On a hot sunny day on the ground, panels can reach 70–80°C. You can also use the Thermal Analysis module to calculate this automatically.
mppt % default: 95.0
MPPT stands for Maximum Power Point Tracking — it's an electronic controller that constantly adjusts to get the maximum possible power from the solar panels. This value is how efficient that controller is. Modern MPPT controllers are 93–98% efficient. If you don't have an MPPT and are connecting panels directly, use 70–80%.
tilt ° default: 0.0
The angle your panel is tilted from flat (horizontal). 0° = lying flat on top of the wing — this is the most common configuration for fixed-wing drones. 90° = panel standing perfectly vertical. Most UAVs use 0° because the wing is horizontal during cruise flight.
panel_2_area & panel_2_tilt m² / ° default: 0 (disabled)
Optional second panel mounted at a different angle — for example, panels on the fuselage sides. Set panel_2_area = 0 to disable this. Only use it when you have panels physically mounted at two different tilt angles.

Panel 2 — Location & Time

lat ° (latitude) default: 13.0 (Bengaluru, India)
Where on Earth you are flying. Positive numbers = north of equator, negative = south. Examples: Bengaluru = 13°, London = 51°, New York = 40°, Sydney = −34°. You can also click on the map to set this automatically. Latitude is the biggest factor in how much solar energy is available.
altitude m above sea level default: 500
The height of your launch site above sea level (not flying altitude — this is ground elevation). Higher ground = thinner atmosphere above you = slightly more solar energy available. Bengaluru is at 900 m, Denver is at 1600 m, sea-level sites = 0. Difference is small (about 1% per 100 m).
month 1–12 default: 6 (June)
The month of your mission. 1 = January, 6 = June, 12 = December. This changes the sun's angle — in summer the sun is higher in the sky and there are more daylight hours. For worst-case planning, use December if you're in the northern hemisphere (winter month), or June if in the southern hemisphere.
time_hrs decimal hours (0–24) default: 12.0 (noon)
The time of day you want to analyse, in decimal hours. Examples: 12.0 = noon, 9.0 = 9 AM, 15.5 = 3:30 PM. This is solar time — noon (12.0) is always when the sun is highest. The charts always show the full day profile from 5 AM to 7 PM, but the Verdict and metric cards show results specifically for this time.
clarity dropdown options: Clear / Standard / Hazy
How clear the sky is at your location. Clear = very clean air (mountains, deserts, high altitude) — maximum solar power. Standard = typical clear day in most cities. Hazy = humid, dusty, or polluted air (coastal tropics, industrial areas) — noticeably less solar power. When in doubt, use Standard.

Panel 3 — Propulsion (Your Motors)

num_motors count default: 4
How many motors your drone has. For a single-motor fixed-wing: 1. For a quad-rotor: 4. For a hybrid VTOL (vertical takeoff, horizontal cruise), count only the motors that run during cruise — typically just the pusher/puller motor, not the vertical lift motors.
cruise_power Watts per motor default: 80 W
How many Watts each motor uses at your normal cruise throttle. This is always in Watts. Not thrust, not RPM — electrical Watts going into the motor. Total motor power = num_motors × cruise_power.

How to find this value: Look at your motor's thrust test data table. Find the row for your cruise throttle (e.g., 30–40%). Multiply the voltage × current shown in that row. Example: 22.2V × 4A = 88.8W per motor.
airspeed km/h default: 60
Your drone's speed through the air (not over the ground). Used to calculate how far the drone can fly before the battery runs out. Most fixed-wing solar drones cruise at 40–80 km/h.
wind_speed_ms m/s default: 0
Headwind speed in metres per second. Flying into a headwind forces the motor to work harder to maintain airspeed, using more power. Set to 0 for calm conditions. Note: 1 m/s ≈ 3.6 km/h. A gentle breeze is 2–4 m/s; strong wind is 8–12 m/s.

Panel 4 — Electronics & Payload

These are all the electrical devices on your drone other than the motors. They draw power continuously regardless of flight speed or solar conditions.

power_fcWdefault: 5W
Flight controller — the autopilot computer. Pixhawk ≈ 2–3W, ArduPilot on Raspberry Pi ≈ 5–8W. Check your autopilot's datasheet for current draw at operating voltage.
power_telWdefault: 3W
Telemetry & communications — the radio link back to the ground station. Standard 433 MHz/900 MHz radio ≈ 1–3W. LTE/4G modem ≈ 5–8W. Satellite link ≈ 10–25W.
power_payloadWdefault: 10W
Mission payload — camera, sensor, or any device you're carrying. RGB camera ≈ 3–8W. Thermal camera ≈ 10–20W. Survey multispectral sensor ≈ 5–15W. Set to 0 for an endurance-only test with no payload.
power_otherWdefault: 2W
Everything else — GPS module, indicator LEDs, servo rail, cooling fans, etc. If you're unsure, add about 10% of your total electronics power here as a safety margin.

Panel 5 — Battery

batt_whWhdefault: 500
Total battery energy in Watt-hours. To calculate it from your pack specs:
Wh = Voltage × Amp-hours
Example: a 6S (22.2V) 10,000 mAh (10 Ah) LiPo = 22.2 × 10 = 222 Wh.
A 6S 50,000 mAh pack = 22.2 × 50 = 1,110 Wh.
batt_chemdropdown
Your battery chemistry. This affects how efficiently energy can be taken out (discharge efficiency) and how long the battery lasts before wearing out. See the comparison table below.
ChemistryBest ForEnergy per kgHow long it lasts
LiPo Most drones, lightweight builds High (~250 Wh/kg) ~400 charge cycles before significant wear
Li-Ion Balance of weight and lifespan Medium (~200 Wh/kg) ~800 cycles
LiFePO4 Long-life missions, daily flying Lower (~130 Wh/kg) ~2,000 cycles — great for HALE drones
min_soc% (minimum charge level)default: 20%
The lowest the battery is allowed to go — a safety reserve. 20% means the drone will try to land before the battery drops below 20%. This energy is never used for flight. Higher reserve = shorter endurance but longer battery life. Recommended: 15–20% for LiPo.
charge_eff%default: 95%
How efficiently the battery can absorb solar energy while charging. Some energy is lost as heat during charging. Most lithium battery systems are 92–97% efficient at charging. If unknown, leave at the default 95%.

Solar Power (W) How many Watts your panels are generating right now at your chosen time Should be higher than Consumption
Consumption (W) How many Watts the whole drone is using (motors + all electronics) Should be lower than Solar Power
Net Balance (W) The difference: Solar Power minus Consumption. Positive = surplus going to battery. Negative = drawing from battery. Positive is good. The bigger the positive number, the more comfortable your margin is.
Endurance (hrs) If the drone is drawing from the battery (negative net balance), this shows how many hours until the battery reaches the minimum level. Shows "—" when you don't need the battery. "—" is the best outcome (solar-powered). Higher numbers are better if battery-limited.

Daily Energy (Wh) Total solar energy harvestable in one full day (5 AM to 7 PM). Think of it as the total "solar income" for the day. Should exceed your drone's daily consumption (Consumption W × 24 hours)

⚠️
Why can "SUSTAINABLE" and "Night Survival: At Risk" appear together?

SUSTAINABLE means your solar panels produce enough power during the day. "At Risk" means your battery is too small to get through the night on stored charge alone. They're checking two different things — daytime solar balance vs night-time battery reserve. You need both to be good for truly 24-hour operations.

5.4 — The Verdict Banner

The large coloured banner is the most important result. It tells you, in one glance, whether your drone design works for the chosen conditions.

✅
SUSTAINABLE
Solar power covers consumption with ≥15% margin

Your solar panels generate enough power to fly indefinitely at this time and location. The battery is actively charging. You're good to go. Check Night Survival if you also need overnight endurance.

⚠️
MARGINAL
Solar covers consumption but margin is less than 15%

Solar is just barely enough. The drone is sustained but the margin is thin — any cloud cover, headwind increase, or slightly higher power draw could tip it into battery mode. Consider increasing panel area by 15–20% or reducing power consumption slightly.

🔋
BATTERY ASSISTED
Solar is not enough, but battery can last 4+ hours

Solar panels alone can't sustain flight right now. The drone is drawing from the battery. However, there's more than 4 hours of battery reserve — enough for a useful mission. Consider flying earlier/later when solar is stronger, or look at the Optimal Launch Window chart.

🚫
INSUFFICIENT
Solar is not enough and battery lasts less than 4 hours

The design doesn't work for these conditions. Solar is far below what's needed, and the battery will run out quickly. You need to significantly increase panel area, reduce power consumption, change the location/month, or fly at a different time of day.

6 Understanding the 12 charts

All charts update live when you change any input. Hover any chart to see a ⬇ PNG button (save the chart as an image) and a ⬇ CSV button (download the data as a spreadsheet). You can zoom in with the scroll wheel.

C1Solar Power vs Time of Day

What it shows: How your solar panel's power output changes through the day, from 5 AM to 7 PM in 30-minute steps.

What's on it:

  • Blue line — your solar panel power at each hour for the current month
  • Red dashed line — total power your drone consumes (constant)
  • Green shaded area — the hours when solar exceeds consumption (battery is charging)
  • Amber line (toggle on/off) — December profile for seasonal comparison

How to read it: The width of the green area = Solar Window (how many hours per day the drone is self-powered). The peak of the blue line = maximum power at noon. If the blue line never crosses the red line, the drone is always battery-limited — you need more panel area.

C2Power Breakdown

What it shows: A horizontal bar chart of where the power is going — how much each system consumes versus how much the solar panels produce.

How to read it: Longer bar = bigger consumer. If the motor bar is very long compared to the solar bar, that's why you have a deficit. This chart is great for spotting which system to optimise first.

C3Battery Level — 24 Hours

What it shows: The battery's state of charge (0–100%) through a complete day and night cycle, starting at midnight.

What to look for:

  • The curve should never touch the red dashed line (minimum SOC) — if it does, the battery runs out at that point
  • During the day (between the sunrise and sunset markers), the curve should rise as the battery charges
  • At night, the curve drops as the battery powers the drone
  • For 24-hour operation: the curve should come back to roughly where it started by the next midnight
C4Battery Level — Multiple Days

What it shows: The same battery simulation extended over 7 days.

How to read it: If the peaks stay the same each day, your energy is balanced. If the peaks slowly drop each day, you're harvesting less solar energy than you're consuming — eventually the drone will run out of power. This shows whether your design is truly sustainable over multi-day missions.

C5Optimal Launch Window

What it shows: Solar power output at every 30-minute slot from 5 AM to 7 PM, alongside the consumption line. Helps you find the best time to launch.

How to use it: If you can't fly all day, launch when the solar curve is clearly above the consumption line — this maximises your solar-powered flight window.

C6NASA Real-World Solar Data Comparison

What it shows: A comparison of this tool's calculated solar energy vs real historical data from NASA's satellite database for your location, month by month through the year.

Why this matters: The tool's model assumes clear-sky conditions. The NASA data includes actual cloud cover from historical records. If the two are far apart, switch your "clarity" setting from Clear to Standard or Hazy to get a more realistic result. Requires an internet connection on first use.

C7Panel Performance Over Years (Degradation)

What it shows: How much your panel's power output will reduce over 10–25 years of use. Solar cells slowly become less efficient over time.

How to read it: The curve starts at 100% and drops gradually. The dashed 80% line marks the industry standard "end of life" — when the panel is considered worn out. The point where the curve crosses 80% is roughly when you'd need to replace the panels. Use the controls to adjust the expected degradation rate.

C8Thermal Analysis — Heat vs Power

What it shows: How the panel's temperature changes through the day (left scale, °C) and how that affects power output (right scale, W). There are two power lines — one showing actual power with temperature effects, and one showing what power would be at a constant 25°C.

Key insight: Hot panels are less efficient. The gap between the two power curves shows how much power you're losing due to heat. In very hot climates or with enclosed panel mounts, this loss can be significant. Flying helps because airflow cools the panels.

C9Battery Lifespan Estimate

What it shows: How your battery's capacity (energy it can hold) decreases over years of charging and discharging cycles.

How to read it: The curve starts at 100% capacity and drops. When it hits the 80% line, the battery is considered worn out and needs replacement. LiFePO4 batteries last much longer than LiPo. Keeping your minimum SOC higher (shallower discharges) dramatically extends battery life.

C10Monte Carlo — Design Reliability Distribution

What it shows: A bar chart showing the spread of possible net power outcomes when all your input parameters vary slightly from their entered values (simulating real-world uncertainty).

How to read it:

  • The vertical line is your current result (baseline)
  • Bars to the right of zero = solar surplus (good)
  • Bars to the left of zero = power deficit (bad)
  • Tall narrow peak = your result is very consistent — design is reliable
  • Wide spread = result varies a lot — design is sensitive to small changes

If most of the bars are on the positive side, your design is robust even under real-world uncertainty.

C11Altitude Effect on Solar Power

What it shows: How solar irradiance and air density change as you go from sea level up to 8,000 metres altitude.

Key insight: Higher altitude = more solar power (less atmosphere blocking the sun) but thinner air (propellers must work harder). This chart helps high-altitude UAV designers find the best operating altitude for their design.

C12Battery Performance in Cold Weather

What it shows: How much usable capacity your battery loses at cold temperatures, for all three chemistry types.

Key insight: LiPo batteries lose 30–50% of their effective capacity at −20°C. LiFePO4 is much more stable in the cold. If you're flying in winter or at high altitude, this chart shows whether your battery needs insulation or pre-heating before flight.

7 Advanced Analysis Tools

📅12-Month Performance Table

What it does: Runs the full calculation for every month of the year and shows the results in one table. This is the most important tool for mission planning — it tells you which months your drone works and which ones it doesn't.

Columns explained:

  • Peak Solar (W) — Maximum panel output during that month (at midday)
  • Daily Energy (Wh) — Total solar energy harvestable in one day
  • Solar Window (hrs) — Hours per day when solar exceeds consumption
  • Endurance (hrs) — Battery-limited flight time for that month ("—" = unlimited/sustainable)
  • Verdict — Green/amber/red verdict for that month
  • Sunrise & Sunset — Daylight hours for that month at your location
  • Min Area (m²) — Smallest panel area needed to break even at noon in that month
💡
Planning tip

Always size your drone for the worst month (December for northern hemisphere, June for southern). If your design is SUSTAINABLE in December, it will work all year round.

⚖️Config Comparison (A vs B)

What it does: Lets you compare two different setups side by side. Enter Config A (your current design) and Config B (a modified version), and see which one wins on each metric.

Example uses: Compare your current panel vs a larger panel. Compare LiPo vs LiFePO4 battery. Compare flying in June vs December. The table shows the delta (difference) and highlights which configuration performs better for each metric.

🌪️Sensitivity Analysis (Tornado Chart)

What it does: Tests how sensitive your result is to each input parameter. It slightly increases and decreases each parameter one at a time (while keeping everything else the same) and shows how much that changes the net power balance.

How to read it: The longest bar = the parameter that matters most for your design. Focus your engineering effort on improving the things with the longest bars. Bars pointing left = increasing that thing makes your design worse. Bars pointing right = increasing it makes it better.

Example: If "panel_area" has the longest bar, adding more panel area will have the biggest positive impact on your design.

🎲Monte Carlo Analysis

What it does: Runs 500 simulations where all parameters are randomly varied at the same time within a set uncertainty range. This answers: "How reliable is this design in the real world, where nothing is perfect?"

How to use it: Set the number of samples (500 is a good start) and the uncertainty percentage (10% means each parameter can vary ±10% from your entered value). Click Run and wait a few seconds.

How to read the results:

  • Sustainable % — Out of 500 runs, how many resulted in a sustainable design? Aim for >80%.
  • P5 value — In the worst 5% of scenarios, what is the net power? If this is still positive, your design is very robust.
  • Histogram chart (C10) — The shape of the distribution. Taller and narrower = more predictable. Wider spread = design is more sensitive to uncertainty.
🗓️Mission Window Planner

What it does: Creates a colour-coded grid showing every combination of month (Jan–Dec) and time of day (5 AM–7 PM). Green = SUSTAINABLE, amber = MARGINAL or BATTERY ASSISTED, red = INSUFFICIENT.

How to use it: Look for the "green rectangle" — that's your safe operating envelope. Schedule your missions within those hours and months. Hover any cell to see the exact net power value at that time/month.

This is the single best tool for deciding when to fly for maximum solar coverage.

🗺️Route Planner (Interactive Map)

What it does: Lets you draw a flight route on the map by clicking waypoints. The tool calculates the solar power and energy balance for each segment of the route separately.

How to use it:

  1. Click on the map to place your first waypoint (take-off point)
  2. Click again to add more waypoints along your route
  3. Click "Calculate Route" to see the energy budget for each segment
  4. Click "Clear Route" to start over

The results table shows distance, solar power, consumption, and verdict for each leg of the flight. The worst segment is highlighted — that's your design-limiting case.

📉Panel Degradation

What it does: Shows how your solar panel's performance will reduce over years of use. Useful for planning long-duration programs (HALE UAVs, research platforms).

Controls: Choose how many years to project (10, 15, 20, or 25 years) and the annual degradation rate. Premium panels degrade at 0.25–0.30% per year; standard panels at 0.45–0.50% per year.

Key output: The year when panel output drops below 80% of original — the standard "replacement needed" milestone.

🌡️Thermal Analysis

What it does: Calculates the actual operating temperature of your solar cells through the day, based on ambient temperature and how the panel is mounted. Then shows the resulting power loss due to heat.

Controls: Set the ambient temperature and select how your panel is mounted (flying UAV = best cooling; building-integrated = worst). The tool shows you the peak cell temperature and how much power you're losing compared to ideal lab conditions (25°C).

🌞Annual Solar Heatmap

What it does: Generates a colour-coded calendar showing the solar energy available for every day of the year. Dark = low energy, bright green/yellow = high energy.

Note: This takes about 10–15 seconds to calculate (it's doing 365 days of calculations). Wait for it to finish before clicking again. Hover any day to see the exact energy value and how it compares to the best day of the year.

🔋Battery Cycle Life

What it does: Estimates how many years your battery will last before losing 20% of its capacity (the standard "worn out" threshold).

Controls: Set how many flights per day you plan, and how many years to project.

Key insight: The deeper you discharge the battery each cycle (lower min SOC), the faster it wears out. Keeping min SOC at 30% instead of 20% can double the battery's lifespan. Use this tool to find the right balance between daily endurance and battery longevity.

☁️Cloud Cover Survival

What it does: Estimates what happens to battery level if a cloud bank reduces solar power for a period during flight.

How to use it: Enter how many hours of cloud cover to expect and how opaque the clouds are (0% = clear, 100% = completely blocked). The tool shows how much battery SOC would drop during that cloud period and whether the drone survives.

However, check Night Survival — with a 500 Wh battery and ~10.5 hours of night at 120 W draw = 1,260 Wh needed. The battery only stores 380 Wh usably, so Night Survival will show "At Risk". For 24-hour operation, you'd need a ~1,500 Wh battery.

Example 2 — The same drone in winter at high latitude (challenging conditions)

Goal: Test the same drone in Germany (52°N) in December. This is the worst-case scenario for Europe.

Change these settings from Example 1

  • Latitude: 52° N (Germany)
  • Month: 12 (December)
  • Clarity: Standard

What you should see

At noon in December at 52°N, the sun is only about 15° above the horizon — very low. The solar power will drop dramatically (perhaps 80–150 W at noon for a 2.5 m² panel). Since consumption is 120 W, the verdict will be MARGINAL or BATTERY ASSISTED.

How to fix it for winter operation:

  1. Increase panel area to 6–8 m² to compensate for the low sun angle
  2. Restrict flying to the 10 AM – 2 PM window when sun is highest (check Mission Window chart)
  3. Reduce payload power to lighten the consumption load
  4. Accept battery-assisted operation and size the battery for 4+ hours endurance

Example 3 — Finding the best launch time for a marginal design

Goal: Your drone gets a MARGINAL verdict at noon. You want to know the safest flight window.

Use the Mission Window Planner (Module 7.5) to see the full colour grid. Find the continuous green band — that is your safe operating window. If it's only green from 09:30 to 14:30, plan your mission to start after 09:30 and land before 14:30.

Also check Chart 1 (Solar Power vs Time) — the hours where the blue line is clearly above the red dashed consumption line are your safe hours. The wider this gap, the more comfortable your operation.

9 Common Questions & Problems

Question or ProblemAnswer
Endurance shows "—" (a dash). Is something broken? No — this is actually the best result. It means your drone is SUSTAINABLE and doesn't need the battery at all. Endurance (how long the battery lasts) only shows a number when the drone is drawing power from the battery.
Range shows "—" even when my drone has a big battery Range only applies when the battery is being used up. When you're SUSTAINABLE, range is effectively unlimited — so the tool shows "—" instead of a misleadingly large number.
The verdict says SUSTAINABLE but Night Survival says "At Risk" These are two different checks. SUSTAINABLE means solar covers daytime power. "At Risk" means the battery isn't large enough to last through the night without solar. You need to increase your battery capacity (batt_wh) for overnight operations.
Solar power seems way too high / too low Check the clarity setting. "Clear" gives maximum solar (pristine mountain air). For most real-world locations, "Standard" is more realistic. Also check your latitude and month — a location near the equator in summer will have much more solar than a northern site in winter.
I changed the month but the verdict didn't change Near the equator, the sun stays high all year and seasonal differences are small. Use the 12-Month Table to see the full range of variation across all months.
The Annual Heatmap is loading very slowly or not loading This is normal — the heatmap runs 365 days of calculations and takes 10–20 seconds. Don't click the button again. Just wait. If it fails, refresh the page and try once more.
The NASA GHI chart shows no data / only one line The NASA data requires an internet connection. If you're offline or behind a firewall, only the model's clear-sky line will appear. The data is cached after the first successful fetch, so it will show on future visits if you were online at least once.
Monte Carlo shows an error Make sure all input fields have a number in them (especially Panel 2 fields). Restart the server if needed: python -m uvicorn app.main:app --port 8000. Also check that the number of samples isn't set too high on a slow computer.
Min Area shows "None" in the monthly table This happens in winter at very high latitudes (like above 60° in December) when the sun barely rises or doesn't rise at all. With zero solar energy available, there is no mathematically valid minimum area — so the tool shows "None".
My saved configuration isn't there anymore Named configurations are stored on the server. If the server was restarted and the database file was lost, configs may be gone. For reliable long-term storage, use the Share URL feature or export to PDF.
I don't know what "Wh" means Wh stands for Watt-hours — a measure of energy. It's like a fuel tank size. 500 Wh means the battery can supply 500 Watts for 1 hour, or 100 Watts for 5 hours, etc. To find your battery's Wh: multiply voltage × amp-hour rating (e.g., 22.2V × 10Ah = 222 Wh).
What is "solar time" and how is it different from local time? Solar time is based purely on the sun's position. Solar noon (12:00) is always when the sun is highest in the sky, regardless of your timezone. It might differ from your clock by up to 1–2 hours. For this tool's purposes, use solar time — so noon is always 12.0, regardless of where you are.
